Good news — you don't need a student visa

EU/EEA citizens don't need a visa, but there's still a short route to follow — including recognizing your academic credentials in Spain.

Within 2 weeks of arrival

Register your address (empadronamiento)

Register at your local town hall — you'll need this for almost every next step.

Within 90 days of arrival

Register with the Oficina de Extranjeros

Bring your passport/ID, proof of address, and proof of funds or enrollment. You'll receive a Certificado de Registro — keep it with your ID for future paperwork.

Before enrolling

Get your academic credentials recognized

Your diploma and transcripts usually need a certified Spanish translation, even for EU credentials — universities ask for this as part of enrollment.

Included with your Complete plan — a short, practical guide for arrival day and the weeks after.

Getting a SIM card

Vodafone, Orange, and Movistar all have kiosks in the airport arrivals area — prepaid tourist/data plans are quick to set up with just your passport. Prices are similar to buying in the city, so it's fine to grab one on arrival rather than hunting for a better deal.

Getting to your accommodation

The Aerobús runs to Plaça Catalunya in about 35 minutes for €6.75. The R2 Nord train is cheaper (~€4.50) and similar in time, but involves a transfer. A taxi is roughly €35–40 and 25–35 minutes, worth it with heavy luggage or a late arrival. Ride-hailing apps work but can be pricier at peak airport times.

Changing money to euros

Airport currency exchange counters have the worst rates you'll see — avoid changing more than you need for a taxi or snacks there. A debit card with no foreign transaction fees, or withdrawing from a bank-branded ATM (not a standalone "Euronet"-style machine), will get you a fairer rate once you're in the city.

Quick snacks and water at the airport

There are supermarkets and cafés past security in both terminals if you land hungry — cheaper than the sit-down options. Tap water in Barcelona is safe to drink, so a refillable bottle saves you buying water once you're settled.

Staying aware of your belongings

Petty theft (mainly pickpocketing) happens more on the metro, Las Ramblas, and around crowded tourist spots than most other parts of the city. Keep bags zipped and in front of you on public transport, keep your phone out of back pockets, and split cash/cards across bags when possible. None of this is unique to Barcelona — normal city awareness is enough.

What to pack

Barcelona is mild year-round but has a real, damp winter — pack for layers, not extremes.

• EU plug adapter (type C/F) • A warm layer for Nov–Feb evenings • Comfortable walking shoes • A physical copy of every roadmap document • Printed proof of health insurance • A bank card with no foreign-transaction fees

Still looking for housing?

You'll need a signed rental contract or host authorization letter for your empadronamiento — a few pointers before you sign anything:

  • Idealista and Badi are the two listing sites most students use — avoid deals that only exist on Facebook groups with no viewing.
  • Never wire a deposit before seeing the place in person or on a verified video call.
  • Gràcia, Sants, and Poble Sec are popular, well-connected areas within reach of most universities.
Browse listings on Idealista ↗

Settling in beyond day one

Learning enough Spanish (or Catalan, day to day here) to feel at ease takes real time — usually longer than people expect, and it's normal to feel isolated or slow-going at first, even if you studied the language before. That adjustment is a genuine part of moving, not a sign you're doing something wrong. Give it months, not weeks, and lean on structured practice and real conversation rather than trying to push through alone.

Escuela Oficial de Idiomas (EOI)

Spain's public language school system — low-cost structured Spanish and Catalan courses, official certificates, evening schedules that work around studies.

EOI Barcelona — course info ↗

Language exchange (intercambio) groups

Informal, usually free meetups pairing you with locals to practice — often the fastest way to build both language and a social circle at the same time.
